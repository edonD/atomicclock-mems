## 💡 Key Physics Concepts Visualized

### 1. Bloch Sphere
- Shows quantum states as points on a sphere
- North pole = F=3 excited state
- South pole = F=2 ground state
- Anywhere on surface = superposition

### 2. Rabi Oscillation
- Population oscillates between states
- Period = 2π / Ω_rabi
- For 100 kHz Rabi: full cycle ≈ 10 μs

### 3. Resonance
- Works best at 6.834 GHz (right on frequency)
- Off-resonance = weaker response
- Detuning creates "AC Stark shift"

### 4. Decoherence
- Quantum superposition decays over time
- Caused by collisions, noise, spontaneous emission
- Limited coherence time (~100-1000 ns for Rb-87)

### 5. Quantum Interference
- Ramsey fringes show interference pattern
- Can distinguish 6.834 GHz ± 100 mHz easily
- This is how atomic clocks measure time!

## 🔬 Real-World Application: Rb-87 Atomic Clock

The NIST Rb-87 atomic clock uses **exactly these physics principles**:

1. **Prepare** superposition with π/2 pulse
2. **Wait** for quantum interference to develop (Ramsey time)
3. **Measure** populations in two different bases
4. **Feedback** to lock oscillator at 6.834 GHz
5. **Result**: Frequency stable to ~0.1 ppm

This same setup powers:
- GPS satellites (frequency accuracy crucial)
- Telecommunications (network synchronization)
- Deep space probes (communication)
- Scientific research (precision measurements)

## ❓ Common Questions

**Q: Why 6.834 GHz?**
A: This is the exact frequency of the F=2 ↔ F=3 hyperfine transition in Rb-87. Frequency defined by nuclear and electron spins.

**Q: Why is it so stable?**
A: Atomic transitions are affected by external fields in very predictable ways. By measuring at the quantum level, you get ~10x better stability than any mechanical oscillator.

**Q: Can this actually measure time?**
A: Yes! By counting oscillations at 6.834 GHz, you can measure time to nanosecond precision. The Rb-87 clock is so stable it wouldn't lose a second in 20,000 years.

**Q: What's the difference between Rabi and Ramsey?**
A: Rabi = single continuous pulse (measures population). Ramsey = two pulses separated by wait time (measures coherence and frequency).

**Q: Why does detuning matter?**
A: Off-resonance driving is inefficient. If your microwave is at 6.835 GHz instead of 6.834 GHz, the atoms respond weakly. This is how we measure frequency errors!
