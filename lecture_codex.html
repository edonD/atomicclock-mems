How I would explain a MEMS atomic clock

Strip away the mystique and it becomes an engineering story: a mediocre local oscillator is constantly compared against a very stubborn quantum reference, and the control loop keeps dragging the electronics back to the right frequency.

A chip-scale atomic clock is not a crystal replacement made out of magic. It is a feedback system whose referee is a cloud of rubidium atoms inside a tiny heated cell.

The local oscillator wants to drift. The atoms do not care about your temperature, board stress, supply noise, or aging. They only respond strongly when the frequency spacing between two optical tones matches their internal hyperfine splitting.

That is the whole idea: find the atomic resonance, measure whether you are left or right of it, then steer the oscillator back onto it forever.

Mental model first

Before equations, get the machine architecture straight. Otherwise the physics feels abstract.

Quartz oscillators are useful because they are cheap, compact, and easy to integrate. They are also imperfect. Their frequency shifts with temperature, stress, package aging, and electronics drift.

Atomic clocks fix that by not trusting the local oscillator alone. They continuously compare it to an atomic transition whose frequency is the same for every correct Rb-87 atom.

local oscillator drifts
compare against atomic resonance
measure error sign
integrate correction
repeat forever
The atom does not generate your final clock signal directly. It tells the electronics whether they are wrong.

The atomic reference

Rubidium-87 gives the clock a frequency target that is set by quantum structure, not by a fab tolerance.

The Lambda system

The important part is not the drawing. It is the logic: two ground states, one excited state, and two coherent optical paths that can interfere.

In Rb-87, the two ground hyperfine states are split by a fixed energy gap. That gap corresponds to a microwave frequency:

fhfs = 6,834,682,610.904 Hz

That number is what the clock cares about. If your interrogation scheme creates two coherent tones whose frequency difference equals that value, the atom responds in a very distinctive way.

A useful way to think about it: the atoms are a frequency template. They do not care about your implementation details. They only care whether the spacing is right.

The clock is anchored to a transition frequency, not to a mechanical resonance in silicon or quartz.

Laser plus sidebands

The laser itself is optical, but the atomic spacing we need to match is microwave. RF modulation bridges that gap.

A VCSEL emits near the rubidium D1 line at 795 nm. By modulating the laser current at roughly half the hyperfine splitting, you create optical sidebands around the carrier. The first-order sidebands are separated by:

Δf = 2 fmod

So if you choose fmod = fhfs / 2, the sideband spacing is exactly what the atom wants.

That is the elegant trick in CPT clocks. You do not synthesize two independent lasers. You use one laser and one RF modulation frequency to build a coherent two-tone probe.

Engineer translation: the RF synthesizer chooses the spacing, and the atom grades whether that spacing is correct.

CPT and the dark state

This is the key physical effect. When the spacing is right, the atom enters a superposition that stops absorbing light.

Interpretation Near the peak, not centered

With only one optical path, the atom absorbs and re-emits light in the ordinary way. With two coherent paths whose spacing matches the hyperfine gap, quantum interference changes the answer.

The atom can be driven into a superposition of the two ground states such that the excitation amplitudes cancel. In that state, the atom stops coupling to the light. That is why it is called a dark state.

right spacing → destructive interference in excitation
dark state forms
absorption drops
photodiode sees more transmitted light

The resonance is narrow. That narrowness is useful because it gives the servo loop a precise target. The sharper the feature, the easier it is to tell whether you are a little bit high or a little bit low.

Practical reading: the vapor cell is acting like a very selective frequency-dependent transparency sensor.

The servo loop

The physics finds the target. The servo is what keeps the clock actually sitting on that target while the real hardware misbehaves.

VCO / oscillatorProduces the tunable clock frequency
→
Laser + MEMS cellConverts frequency error into optical transmission change
→
Photodiode + controllerMeasures error and pushes correction back

If you lock to the top of a peak, you face a classic problem: at the exact center the slope is zero, so how do you know which side you are on? The normal answer is dither.

You wiggle the oscillator slightly, measure how transmission changes, and demodulate that response. The sign of that demodulated signal tells you whether you are above or below resonance.

dither frequency
measure transmission change
extract error sign
integrate correction into oscillator tuning word

This is why I frame the whole clock as a control system. The resonance alone is not enough. Without the loop, you only have a nice measurement. With the loop, you have a stable clock.

How this repo turns the dip into a clock

This project implements a feedback chain. The photodiode trace is the measurement, and the corrected oscillator is the thing that becomes the clock reference.

If you were confused by the dip plot, this is the missing bridge: the project does not treat the dip itself as the clock output. It uses the dip as a frequency error sensor.

1. 06_rf_synthesis PLL + VCO

Generate a tunable RF tone near 3.417 GHz, which is half the Rb-87 hyperfine splitting.

2. 01_vcsel_sideband VCSEL modulation

Use that RF tone to modulate the laser so the first-order optical sidebands are separated by 6.834 GHz.

3. 00_atomic_model CPT dip

The rubidium model produces the narrow CPT resonance and the discriminator slope that tells the controller how wrong the oscillator is.

4. 05_optical Photodiode

Convert transmitted light into photodiode current and SNR. This is the measurement path, not the exported clock.

5. 07_servo_loop PID lock

Use the CPT slope and VCO gain to build the control loop that keeps pulling the oscillator back to resonance.

6. 08_allan + 09_fullchain Clock quality

Evaluate how stable the locked oscillator is once shot noise, VCO noise, and thermal drift are combined.

The key sentence is: photodiode output = error information, oscillator output = the clock reference.

Why MEMS matters

The atomic principle is old. The hard product work is making the vapor cell tiny, manufacturable, warm, and stable enough to use on a chip.

A simplified chip cross-section

Top optical windowLets the VCSEL beam enter the cavity
Rb-87 vapor + N2 buffer gasThe active medium where CPT happens
Silicon cavity wallsMicrofabricated geometry that defines the cell
Microheater + temperature sensorKeeps vapor pressure and operating point stable
Package / magnetic shieldingReduces environmental disturbance
None of these pieces are decorative. Each exists because a different noise or instability source has to be pushed down.

Lab atomic clocks can be large because they have space for optics, thermal control, and shielding. A MEMS clock tries to compress the same logic into a package small enough to deploy.

The cell must be hot enough to get the right rubidium vapor density, but heating burns power. Buffer gas reduces motion-related broadening, but too much gas shifts and broadens the line. Small geometry saves volume, but it increases wall interactions and tightens thermal constraints.

So MEMS is not just miniaturization. It is the discipline of keeping the useful physics alive while every dimension, milliwatt, and fabrication tolerance gets tighter.

The product challenge is to preserve atomic-quality timing inside a package that looks and behaves like an ordinary embedded component.

What controls stability

Allan deviation is the clock world's way of asking: over a given averaging time, how repeatable is the frequency?

You do not need the full statistics first. Start with the engineering intuition: the clock gets better when the resonance is narrow, the contrast is high, the photodetector signal is clean, and the servo is quiet and well-tuned.

better stability comes from:
narrower CPT linewidth
higher contrast
better signal-to-noise ratio
lower environmental sensitivity
cleaner control loop

Optical noise

Laser intensity noise, frequency noise, and poor sideband balance all blur the error signal. If the resonance feature looks fuzzy, the loop cannot lock sharply.

Cell physics noise

Temperature shifts, buffer-gas effects, wall collisions, and magnetic fields perturb the transition or broaden the dark resonance.

Control and electronics noise

Phase noise in the synthesizer, noisy readout electronics, and a sloppy servo loop can turn a good resonance into a mediocre output clock.

If you want the shortest honest summary: an atomic clock is only as good as the whole chain from laser modulation to cell physics to photodetection to servo correction.
