Part 1: Rubidium-87 Ground State Energy Levels

What Is An Atom?

An atom is a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons. The electrons sit in discrete energy levels — they can't have just any energy, only specific allowed values.

Rubidium-87 has:

  • 37 protons in the nucleus
  • 50 neutrons in the nucleus
  • 37 electrons orbiting (one per proton)
  • Nuclear spin I = 3/2 (the nucleus itself is spinning)

The outermost (valence) electron is the one that matters for your atomic clock.

Energy Levels of Rb-87

The vertical axis shows energy. Higher up = higher energy state. The energy differences are what produce the frequencies we measure.

Ground State Hyperfine Split

The ground state (5S₁/₂) is split into two levels by the interaction between the electron's spin and the nucleus's spin. This is called hyperfine structure.

Energy difference between F=2 and F=1:
ΔE = h × 6,834,682,610.904 Hz

where h is Planck's constant.

This energy difference always produces a frequency of exactly 6.835 billion Hz — determined by fundamental physics, not by humans.

KEY INSIGHT: The nucleus and electron have intrinsic angular momentum (spins). When they couple, they create two possible energy states: F=2 (high energy) and F=1 (low energy). The energy gap between them is always 6,834,682,610.904 Hz (Planck's equation: E = hf).
Part 2: Where Does 6.835 GHz Come From?

The Physics: Hyperfine Interaction

Hyperfine interaction = magnetic moment of the electron's spin couples to magnetic moment of the nucleus's spin.

Hyperfine energy splitting:
ΔE = A × I · J
where A = hyperfine coupling constant (depends on Rb-87 only)
I = nuclear spin (3/2)
J = electron angular momentum (1/2)

For Rb-87 specifically, this interaction produces a frequency of 6,834,682,610.904 Hz.

This number was measured by NIST scientists in the 1970s. It's so precise that the international second is defined as 9,192,631,770 cycles of Cs-133 (similar principle, different atom), and Rb-87 is calibrated against that. The 6.835 GHz frequency is exactly known to 12 decimal places.

Part 3: Why This Frequency Is Invariant

Why 6.835 GHz Specifically?

You cannot choose a different frequency. Nature sets it.

The frequency is determined by:

  • Planck's constant h (universal)
  • Electron spin magnetic moment (fundamental)
  • Nuclear spin magnetic moment (depends on nucleus)
  • Rb-87 atomic number & mass (fixed)

Once you pick Rb-87, the frequency is locked in. You can't adjust it.

Part 4: How Your VCO "Locks" to 6.835 GHz

The Servo Loop Explanation

Your VCO on the chip oscillates at some starting frequency (a guess). The servo loop automatically tunes it to match the Rb-87 resonance:

Step 1: VCO starts at wrong frequency

VCO is oscillating, but not yet at 6.835 GHz. Maybe it's at 6.834 GHz or 6.836 GHz.

Step 2: Laser is modulated at VCO frequency

The VCO's frequency controls the laser sidebands. If VCO is wrong, the laser frequency is wrong.

Step 3: Atoms detect the mismatch

If the laser frequency doesn't match the atomic transition, atoms absorb light normally. The photodetector signal is high.

Step 4: PID controller sees the error

The PID logic says: "Photodetector signal is too high. Atoms are absorbing. We're off-resonance. Adjust the VCO."

Step 5: DAC adjusts varactor bias

The DAC output tunes the VCO's varactor capacitor. VCO frequency changes slightly.

Step 6: Loop repeats until lock

When VCO frequency = Rb-87 resonance frequency (6.835 GHz), atoms go into the dark state. Photodetector signal drops to minimum. PID sees zero error. VCO stops tuning. LOCKED!

The Rb atoms themselves are the frequency reference. The VCO tunes until it matches the atomic transition. The atoms "tell" the PID controller whether it's on-frequency or off-frequency via the photodiode signal.

Part 7: Why This Frequency Is Special

Why 6.835 GHz and Not 10 GHz or 1 GHz?

Three reasons:

1. Physics (Non-negotiable)

Rb-87's hyperfine gap is 6.835 GHz. That's what nature produces. You can't change it.

2. Engineering (Practical)

6.8 GHz is in the "microwave" band — easy to generate and manipulate with CMOS circuits. Much easier than optical clocks (visible light, 10¹⁴ Hz) or longer wavelengths.

3. Linewidth (Performance)

With N₂ buffer gas at 76.6 Torr, the Rb-87 resonance has a linewidth of 3 kHz. This produces a Q-factor of 2.3 million — extremely sharp resonance → extremely stable clock.

Q-factor (sharpness):
Q = f / Δf = 6,835,000,000 / 3,000 = 2.3 × 10⁶
