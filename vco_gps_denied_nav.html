How the VCO Becomes a Navigation Reference

1. VCO Generates 6.835 GHz

Your CSAC's VCO oscillates at exactly 6,834,682,610.904 Hz, locked to the Rb-87 hyperfine transition. This frequency is invariant — determined by atomic physics, not by electronics.

f = Δf_Rb-87 = 6,834,682,610.904 Hz (by definition)

The stability is 8.84×10⁻¹² per second. This means over 1 second, the frequency could drift by at most 0.00000060 Hz (6 parts in 10 billion).

2. Frequency Divider Creates 1 Hz

A chain of binary counters divides 6.835 GHz down to 1 Hz:

6,835 GHz ÷ 2³³ = 6,835,000,000,000 ÷ 8,589,934,592 ≈ 1 Hz

Key insight: The 1 Hz output is just as stable as the original 6.835 GHz. Dividing doesn't degrade stability; every tick is perfectly spaced in time.

3. INS Uses 1 Hz Ticks as Time Reference

The Inertial Navigation System measures accelerations (from accelerometers) and integrates them over time:

position = position + velocity × Δt + 0.5 × acceleration × Δt²

If Δt is wrong (clock drifts), the position calculation is wrong. With your atomic clock, Δt is accurate to 8.84 parts per trillion, so INS errors stay tiny.

4. Without GPS, Clock Becomes the Truth

When GPS is jammed or unavailable:

  • IMU measures acceleration continuously
  • At each 1 Hz tick, calculate: new_velocity = old_velocity + acceleration
  • At each 1 Hz tick, calculate: new_position = old_position + velocity
  • Time itself is the only external reference (the atomic clock)

Error source: IMU measurement noise (accelerometer bias/drift) + clock frequency drift both contribute. With your clock, frequency drift is negligible; IMU noise dominates.
